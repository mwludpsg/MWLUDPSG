Local Universe and Time Domain Astronomy Laboratory

The ΛCDM model has provided a robust framework for understanding the formation of cosmic structures through the collapse of primordial density fluctuations. While it has been remarkably successful in describing the Universe on large scales, it faces challenges on smaller scales. The Milky Way and the Local Universe offer invaluable insights into addressing these issues.

The project “Mapping the Milky Way–Local Universe Disk Population Structures and Galactoseismology” (MWLUDPSG), leveraging large sky surveys, holds the potential to significantly advance our understanding of the structure and formation history of the Milky Way and the Local Group. With the support of extensive surveys such as 2MASS, SDSS, RAVE, Pan-STARRS, LAMOST, APOGEE, DES, TESS, and WEAVE, alongside future missions like ELT-Mosaic, 4MOST, JUST, MUST, Mephisto, WFST, LSST, and CSST, we are entering a golden age of Galactic and Local Universe studies. These efforts promise groundbreaking insights into the formation history and structure of our home galaxy and other Local Universe disk galaxies, such as M31 and NGC 4013, as well as Milky Way analogs like NGC 891. By observing multiple stellar populations in six-dimensional phase space, these surveys will enable detailed investigations of the Milky Way’s dynamics through chemical tagging, time tagging, kinematics, and dynamics.

The Milky Way is a highly complex dynamical system shaped by interactions with bars, spiral structures, warp, giant molecular clouds, dark matter subhalos, the Gaia–Enceladus-Sausage, the Sagittarius stream, the LMC, the SMC, and tidally disrupted satellite galaxies. These processes imprint clear signatures on stars, leading to rich population structures and galactic seismic signals. This project will cover progress on various aspects, from bulge and disk dynamics to flares, warps, truncations, and rotation curves, as well as stellar streams, matter fields, dwarf galaxies, M31, low-redshift galaxy physics, etc.
